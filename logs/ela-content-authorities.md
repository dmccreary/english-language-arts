# Organizations That Determine US High School English/Language Arts Content

In the US, no single authority dictates ELA content. The main organizations and bodies that shape it:

## National / Multi-State Standards

- **Common Core State Standards Initiative (CCSSI)** — developed by the **National Governors Association (NGA)** and the **Council of Chief State School Officers (CCSSO)**. The Common Core ELA standards are the most influential framework; ~40 states still use them or a lightly rebranded version.
- **National Council of Teachers of English (NCTE)** and the **International Literacy Association (ILA)** — jointly publish the *Standards for the English Language Arts*, which informed Common Core.

## State-Level (the Legal Authority)

Each **State Board of Education** / **State Department of Education** formally adopts the standards used in its public schools. Examples:

- Texas (TEKS)
- Virginia (SOL)
- Florida (B.E.S.T.)
- California (CA CCSS-ELA)

States can adopt Common Core, modify it, or write their own.

## Assessment Organizations (De Facto Influence)

- **College Board** — SAT and AP English Language & AP English Literature frameworks.
- **ACT, Inc.** — ACT English/Reading.
- **Smarter Balanced** and **PARCC** (historically) — Common Core–aligned state tests.
- **NAEP** (the "Nation's Report Card"), governed by the **National Assessment Governing Board**.

## Local

**Local school districts** and individual schools choose curricula, textbooks, and reading lists within the state standards — which is why book selections vary widely even under identical standards.

## Summary

States legally decide, but in practice **Common Core (NGA/CCSSO)** plus **College Board/ACT** assessments drive what most US high school ELA classrooms actually cover.
